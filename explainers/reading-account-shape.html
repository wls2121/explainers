Reading the shape of an account

How to tell whether a customer is sticky and where it can grow — from a couple of usage numbers, no SQL required.

You're good at the selling. This guide is about one thing that trips up everyone new to data: the headline usage number — "models built" — looks like it tells you how valuable and stuck-in a customer is. It doesn't. Two customers can show the same giant number and be completely different bets. Here's how to tell them apart in about five minutes.

Four words, in plain terms

You'll hear these constantly. Here's each one, with a picture.

Model

One transformation step: raw data goes in, a clean table comes out. A model is just a piece of SQL with a name; dbt runs it and saves the result as a table in the warehouse.

Project

The folder of code those models live in — and models depend on each other, so a project is really a web of them. An arrow means "this one is built from that one." A customer can have one project or many.

Environment

A "where it runs" setting — a developer's sandbox, an automated test, or production. Same models, same code; an environment just says where this run happens.

This is the one people get wrong: an environment is a where, not a who. If a dashboard says "60 environments," that is not 60 of their customers.

Run (or build)

One execution. A run tells dbt to build the models once, in dependency order. Every model it builds counts as a "build" — and the headline usage number is just all of those added up over months.

Why the big number lies

A customer's usage number gets large for three different reasons. Which one it is tells you almost everything about whether the account is safe:

usage = breadth × fan-out × cadence

  • Breadth — they model a lot of different things (a big web of steps).
  • Fan-out — they run the same work separately for many of their own customers.
  • Cadence — they just re-run the same thing very often, because the data goes stale fast.

Here's the catch: cadence is cheap, and almost everyone with anything close to real-time has a lot of it. Breadth and fan-out are what make a customer hard to replace. So a big number only means "sticky" when breadth or fan-out is driving it. When cadence is driving it, the number is mostly motion — and a smart engineer (or a competitor) can make most of it disappear without losing anything that matters.

Don't get fooled

  • A big usage number is not the same as a sticky account.
  • "50 environments" is not 50 customers — environments are run-stages.
  • Lots of projects can be a company's internal sprawl, not a product they sell.

The three shapes, and what to do about each

Real examples below. For each: what the business is, what dbt does for them, how to spot the shape, and what it means for your deal.

Deep graph sticky

Example: a marketing-personalization platform. It turns shopper clicks into the audiences and predictions behind many retailers' campaigns. dbt builds the big web of models that feeds the product. The usage is huge because they genuinely model a lot — roughly 500 steps every run.

In plain terms: the projects and environments here are just code and run-stages. Their own customers aren't projects — they're separate datasets sitting downstream in the warehouse.

30-second tell

A high number of steps per run, with only a handful of copies. Big web, not many duplicates.

What it means for your deal

Durable. The work is genuinely hard to rebuild somewhere else. Expand into more teams and use cases. If they grumble about cost, don't panic — the depth is real value; help them tune it, but the account isn't going anywhere quickly.

Fan-out sticky, with a catch

Example: a vertical SaaS serving thousands of small operators. dbt powers the live, in-product analytics each operator sees. It's the same modest pipeline copied across many source systems and customer groups, refreshed hourly. The usage is high because of all the copies, not because any one is big.

In plain terms: each box is a copy of similar logic, not a customer. Their 3,000 end-customers are rows inside the copies. The number is big because of the copies and the hourly refresh — not because any one copy is large.

30-second tell

Many similar projects or jobs, each modest in size, often refreshed hourly. Lots of duplicates of the same thing.

What it means for your deal

Durable, because it sits inside a product their customers depend on — and it grows as their customer base grows, which is the best kind of expansion. The catch: this shape often has heavy usage on very few seats, which can spark a pricing disagreement. Get ahead of that, and look for chances to help them tidy the duplication.

Refresh mill fragile

Example: a real-time lead marketplace. A record is worthless seconds after it arrives, so one small pipeline gets re-run every ~96 seconds. The usage looks enormous, but it's a tiny thing run constantly — almost no breadth, almost no copies.

In plain terms: there's very little here — a small pipeline, no copies. The reason it's a big number is purely how often it re-runs. There's a setting (only rebuild what changed, rather than everything) that could shrink this — and shrink the bill with it.

30-second tell

Small size, few copies, but a very high run frequency. Lots of motion, not much there.

What it means for your deal

Fragile. The big number is mostly busywork an engineer can optimize away — and if usage is how we price them, that shrinks the deal. Don't read the number as safety. Get in front of it: offer to help them run more efficiently before they find out on their own and pull back.

Read any account in five minutes

  1. What does the business do — and does dbt feed something their customers see, or just internal reporting?
  2. Is the big number coming from breadth (lots of different steps), fan-out (many copies), or cadence (frequent re-runs)? If you can't see it, your SA or CSM can pull three numbers: steps-per-run, project/environment counts, and runs-per-day.
  3. Ask yourself: if their re-run frequency dropped tomorrow, would the value mostly stay (sticky) or mostly vanish (fragile)?
  4. Don't count environments as customers.
  5. Match it to a shape above, and use the "what it means for your deal" line.

The one-sentence version

A big usage number can mean a customer is deeply built-in, or it can mean they're doing the same small thing over and over — and those are opposite situations. Look at the shape, not just the size.

The numbers and examples here are real but kept simple, pulled from internal usage analytics. You don't need to reproduce them — your SA or CSM can get the steps-per-run, project and environment counts, and run frequency for any account you're working.
